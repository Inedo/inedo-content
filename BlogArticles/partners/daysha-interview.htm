Catching up with Chris Dunne from Daysha

Last time I was in Dublin, I shared a Guinness (or a few) with Chris Dunne from Daysha Consulting, one of our Services & Training partners. Given his experiences in the DevOps community, I thought it'd be the perfect opportunity for another quick interview!

Thanks for making the time today for a quick chat, it was great to finally get to meet you in person.

Chris: You too!

So how did you end up as a consultant for DevOps and agile practices?

Chris: : Well, I stared off my career in embedded systems, I decided to leave the hard-coded world to start my own company that made a software product for content management and web-applications. As I was working on that I had a bit of free time, and through serendipity was offered a consulting gig, and I’ve been doing it ever since. For about 10 years now actually

With all that diverse experience, you stuck with consulting?

Chris: : It wasn’t something I had planned, or set out to do, I kinda stumbled into it. For me consulting has two sides: on one hand you’re educating the client, and on the other you’re getting project work done. I really liked the mix of the two. I wasn’t expecting that going in, but I really do enjoy that facet of the job.

"On one hand you’re educating the client, and on the other you’re getting project work done..."

With your past and experience I image there is a whole list of stuff you can help out with, what’s your speciality?

Chris: Honestly, it’s DevOps. I know that’s a loaded term, but it really does encompass many of the aspects of the consulting I had been engaged in before the term even became known.

To be a bit more specific I focus on tools consulting, and helping organizations make the most of the tools available to improve how they deliver software to market.

Which is, of course, how we first got on each other’s radar.

Chris: Indeed, I ran across BuildMaster while working for a client.

What is it you hope a client takes away after working with you?

Chris: I hope that they have enjoyed working with us. This means that we have established a good relationship, and they don’t feel like they’ve been ripped off or have wasted time. But mostly, I hope we left the client in a better position than when we started.

You work with a lot of big, multi-national clients, what are they asking for these days?

"Lots of times in the software industry, companies move towards a new trend at break-neck speed"

Chris: Containerization seems to be the biggest conversation starter right now.

Is that good or bad?

Chris: It’s not inherently bad, but I think there is too much hype and not enough true understanding of how containers fit into an overall enterprise architecture.

Lots of times in the software industry, companies move towards a new trend at break-neck speed, for better or worse, and while that can have positive outcomes, it can also cause a lot of reinventing of the wheel and duplication of effort.

Sounds like you’ve seen this pattern before.

Chris: It might sound a little cynical but I do see many organizations adopting new technologies for the sake of it, or at least without a good business reason. Just because something is new it’s not necessarily better, and new doesn’t always mean progress.

I see this especially in the DevOps space where organizations feel pressured to adopt new practices and changes without really understanding what’s wrong with the old processes first.

That must be frustrating.

Chris: Actually it can be really rewarding! Getting an organization to think about where they are before thinking about where they are going can really lead to exceptional outcomes.

I know you follow a lot of different conference presenters, and speakers to stay up-to date, have you come across anything really great lately?

Chris: This year’s Atlassian Fire-side chat, where Atlassian founders talked about their origins and culture. I think Atlassian is a brilliant model for software companies and we can learn a lot from them. It will be really interesting to see how their culture is tested and changed now that they have an IPO under their belt.

Quick! What do you think is the most over-used buzz-word right now?

Chris: Docker

And... we’re right back to containers I see! So let’s wrap this up with one last question: if you could dictate one rule that every dev shop in the world had to follow, what would it be?

Chris: For all green-field projects, automate all testing from day one. Use TDD to help design better software.

Always a good rule. Thanks again for making the time Chris!

Chris: Happy to, this was fun.
